Clio — AI Investment Assistant

Dense financial data, made human. A case study in progressive disclosure, behavioural nudges, and designing for people who don’t speak the language of markets — yet.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Platform
iOS & Android
Duration
8 Weeks
Tools
Figma, Adobe XD, Maze

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Design a portfolio management feature for Clio that makes complex financial data understandable for novice investors without dumbing it down for experienced ones.

The challenge wasn’t purely visual. It was cognitive — how do you show volatility, risk exposure, sector allocation, and smart trade recommendations to someone who opened their first investment account three weeks ago, without overwhelming them into inaction?

“When I open my portfolio I see a wall of numbers and feel like I’m missing something. I just close the app.”

The Problem

Information density is not the same as clarity

Most investment apps were designed for people who already understand finance. They cram every metric onto one screen because power users want it. But for everyone else, it triggers decision paralysis — too much information, no action at all.

This isn’t a cosmetic problem. It’s a retention and trust problem. Users who feel confused churn. Users who feel empowered invest more and stay longer.

Key pain points

  • Dashboard shows 14+ data points simultaneously with no visual hierarchy
  • No contextual explanation of what numbers mean or what to do with them
  • Trade recommendations appear with no rationale, creating distrust
  • Risk levels displayed as jargon-heavy scores (e.g. “Beta 1.4”)
  • No sense of progress or portfolio health at a glance

Research

Listening before designing

User Interviews

12 sessions with novice investors and 6 with experienced traders. Goal: map the knowledge gap and identify where confidence collapses.

Competitive Analysis

Evaluated 5 platforms including Zerodha, Groww, and Robinhood on information hierarchy, onboarding clarity, and trade decision support.

Cognitive Load Mapping

Task-based test asking users to answer 5 simple questions using their current app. Measured time-to-answer and error rates to find overload points.

Key insight: novice users don’t need less data — they need data presented with context and intent. They want to know what something means for them, not just what it is.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Define the user spectrum

Created two primary personas: “Curious Priya” (first-time investor, wants simplicity) and “Confident Raj” (2+ years, wants density). The design needed to serve both without compromise.

02

Progressive disclosure architecture

Restructured information hierarchy so the default view shows only three high-value signals: portfolio health score, today’s change, and a single action nudge. All detail is one tap away.

03

Smart Trade flows

Designed an AI-powered recommendation system that shows not just what to trade but why — with plain-language rationale, risk profile alignment, and a “what happens if I do/don’t” framing.

04

Contextual data visualisation

Replaced raw charts with annotated visuals. Every significant data point has a tooltip in plain language. Sector allocation shows as a labelled visual map, not just percentages.

05

Usability testing and iteration

Two rounds of moderated testing with 8 participants using Maze. Iterated on the Smart Trade confirmation flow after discovering users wanted one more step of reassurance before committing.

Learnings

What this project taught me

Simplicity is not reduction

Hiding information is not the same as designing for clarity. Progressive disclosure works because it respects the user’s journey — giving them what they need, when they need it.

Context is the design

The most impactful change wasn’t visual — it was adding plain-language context to every data point. Data without meaning is noise. Data with meaning is a conversation.

Trust is designed, not assumed

The Smart Trade flow taught me that AI recommendations need to show their work. Users won’t act on a suggestion they don’t understand, no matter how accurate it is.

Test with real sceptics

Recruiting participants who actively avoided investing gave us the most actionable feedback. Designing for the hesitant user makes the product better for everyone.

Amazon Assure

Post-purchase anxiety is a silent churn driver. This feature case study tackles what happens after the buy button — and why that moment defines long-term customer trust.

Role
Lead UX Designer
Platform
iOS, Android, Web
Duration
6 Weeks
Tools
Figma, Hotjar, Lookback

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Design a trust-building feature for Amazon that reduces post-purchase anxiety, lowers WISMO (Where Is My Order) contact rates, and improves customer satisfaction without adding friction to the existing checkout flow.

“I bought it three days ago. I just keep refreshing the tracking page every hour hoping something has changed.”

The Problem

The experience falls apart after the sale

The invisible problem with e-commerce is that the experience falls apart the moment the customer pays. The product is sold. The design team moves on. But the user is now in a state of uncertainty that can last days — and that uncertainty is where trust is won or lost.

Key pain points

  • Return initiation requires 5+ steps with confusing policy language at each point
  • Order tracking shows carrier codes and depot names with no plain-language status
  • No proactive communication when delays occur
  • No visual confirmation of what the guarantee covers until a problem arises
  • Customer support chat used mostly for questions the UI could answer

Research

Finding the trust gaps

Customer Journey Mapping

Mapped the post-purchase journey for 20 users from order confirmation to receipt, identifying every moment of uncertainty and every touch point that either built or eroded trust.

Review Mining

Analysed 500 one-star reviews across product categories. 60% mentioned order uncertainty, return difficulty, or lack of communication as the primary complaint.

Competitive Benchmarking

Evaluated return and tracking UX across Flipkart, Myntra, and Nykaa to identify what the market was doing well and where Amazon had an opportunity to lead.

Core insight: users didn’t need faster delivery. They needed fewer moments of uncertainty. Every step that told a user “here’s where your order is and what happens next” reduced anxiety, even if the timeline didn’t change.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Map the trust gaps first

Mapped all post-confirmation touch points on a trust scale before designing a single screen. This focused effort on the highest-anxiety moments, not the most visible ones.

02

Design the guarantee badge system

A persistent visual badge communicating what protection the customer has, visible on order confirmation, tracking, and product pages.

03

Redesign the tracking page

Replaced carrier codes and depot names with a plain-language progress narrative: “Your order left the sorting facility in Pune and is on its way to Mumbai.”

04

Proactive delay communication

In-app notification system that pre-empts customer queries by proactively communicating delays with reason, updated ETA, and a one-tap support option.

05

Streamlined return initiation

Reduced return initiation from 5 steps to 2 with plain-language policy summaries at the decision point.

Learnings

What this project taught me

The experience lives after the sale

Most design attention goes to conversion. This project proved that the post-purchase experience has enormous leverage on NPS, repeat purchase rate, and brand trust.

Proactive beats reactive

A single proactive delay notification eliminated the need for customers to contact support. Prevention is always cheaper than resolution.

Plain language is a design decision

Changing “HYD1 → BOM2” to a human sentence was one of the highest-impact changes in the project. Clarity is not a copywriting task — it is a UX task.

Guarantees need to be visible to work

Customers didn’t know Amazon’s return policy was generous because it was buried. Making the guarantee visible changed perception before any problem even occurred.

Tryfit — Shopping App

Clothing returns cost the industry billions annually. Most are caused by a single unsolvable problem — people can’t tell if something will fit. This case study is the design response to that.

Role
UX Designer & Researcher
Platform
iOS Mobile App
Duration
10 Weeks
Tools
Figma, Adobe XD, Lookback

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Design a mobile shopping experience that reduces size-related return rates, increases first-purchase conversion, and builds enough confidence in fit prediction that users are willing to buy clothing they’ve never tried on.

“I’ve bought the same jeans in three different sizes hoping one fits. Two of them are still in the bag.”

The Problem

Uncertainty lives between the photo and the body

Clothing e-commerce has a returns problem that’s been accepted as a cost of doing business. But the problem isn’t logistics — it’s uncertainty. Users know what they see; they don’t know how it will feel. The gap between the product image and the lived experience is where conversion dies.

Key pain points

  • Size charts are generic and use measurements most users don’t know without a tape measure
  • Product images show models with unknown body proportions — useless as a fit reference
  • No way to compare how an item fits someone with similar measurements
  • Review sections don’t surface fit information prominently
  • Wishlist and comparison flows are cumbersome, preventing considered decisions

Research

Listening before designing

Generative Research

15 semi-structured interviews with users aged 22–38. Goal: understand the mental model of “deciding to buy” clothing online and where confidence collapses.

Usability Baseline Testing

Task-based testing on the existing prototype. Measured task completion, error rate, and time-on-task for five key journeys including size selection and purchase.

Persona Development

Built three validated personas: The Overcautious Returner, The Optimistic Guesser, and The Research-Heavy Buyer. Each had distinct decision patterns the design needed to accommodate.

Pivotal insight: users weren’t primarily worried about whether something would look good. They were worried about whether it would feel good — fit around the shoulders, length, fabric weight. These are tactile questions no image can answer. The design task was to find proxies for touch.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Map the decision moment

Identified the exact point where confidence collapsed: the size selection screen. Redesigned it as the primary trust-building moment in the entire flow.

02

Smart fit profiles

One-time body measurement flow that maps users to a “fit type” and surfaces real customer reviews filtered by similar body type.

03

Social fit proof

Added a “Fits like” section to every product page, showing verified buyer photos filtered by similar fit type. More useful than professional model shots.

04

Review surface redesign

Moved “fit mentions” from buried review text to a prominent visual breakdown at the top: “Runs small / True to size / Runs large” with percentage breakdowns.

05

Micro-interactions for confidence

Subtle animation states on the size recommendation to communicate that the suggestion was personalised, not generic. Small signal, large impact on perceived accuracy.

Learnings

What this project taught me

Tactile problems need social proxies

You can’t replace touch with technology yet. But you can replace uncertainty with social proof from people who have the same body. That proximity is more persuasive than any feature.

Personalised framing builds trust

The moment a recommendation feels calculated for you rather than averaged from a spreadsheet, trust increases dramatically.

Surface the right reviews, not just the best ones

A review from someone with similar measurements saying “runs small on the shoulders” is worth more than five five-star reviews that don’t mention fit.

Less is more at the decision moment

The original size screen had 7 options and a size guide link. The redesign had a single confident recommendation with an explanation. Confidence is a design output.

CodeVault — Learn to Code

Interview prep is one of the most high-stakes learning contexts that exists — and most tools for it are terrible. CodeVault is the design response to a broken experience.

Role
Product Designer
Platform
iOS Mobile App
Duration
7 Weeks
Tools
Figma, Principle, UserTesting

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Design a mobile learning companion for developers preparing for technical interviews that fits into the real constraints of a working professional’s day — commutes, lunch breaks, distracted evenings — while still building deep, testable knowledge.

“I bought a course, got three hours in, and then work got busy. Now I feel too behind to start again.”

The Problem

Most prep tools were built for people with four hours a day

Most interview prep platforms were designed for people with four hours a day and a whiteboard. The reality is most candidates prep in fifteen-minute windows between meetings, on their phones, with limited concentration. The design had to meet them there.

Key pain points

  • Long-form video content can’t be consumed in short, interrupted sessions
  • No sense of daily progress — the course feels the same length at 10% or 80%
  • Problems are presented without the contextual knowledge needed to solve them
  • No spaced repetition — concepts learned are forgotten by interview day
  • Competitive leaderboards create anxiety rather than motivation

Research

Listening before designing

Learner Interviews

Spoke with 14 developers who had recently gone through interview prep. Mapped the emotional arc from initial motivation through the plateau phase where most people quit.

Learning Science Review

Researched spaced repetition, retrieval practice, and microlearning principles and identified which patterns from education research applied specifically to technical interview prep.

Platform Analysis

Evaluated LeetCode, AlgoExpert, Neetcode, and Grokking on engagement design, progress communication, and mobile experience quality.

Key insight: motivation to prep peaks at the start and collapses at “the plateau” — around 30–40% through, when easy problems are done and hard ones feel impossible. The design needed to address the plateau, not just the first session.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Daily tracks, not courses

Restructured content from “courses to complete” to “daily tracks to do.” Each track takes 12–15 minutes and covers one concept end-to-end. The sense of completion resets every day.

02

Spaced repetition engine

A review system that resurfaces concepts based on how confidently the user rated their understanding. Problems marked “shaky” come back in 24 hours. Problems marked “solid” come back in a week.

03

Contextual problem sets

Every problem is preceded by a 90-second concept primer that gives just enough context to attempt it. No more arriving at a problem cold.

04

Streak and milestone design

Streak system that rewards consistency over volume. Missing one day breaks the streak but doesn’t reset progress — a deliberate choice to reduce the “might as well quit” response.

05

Offline-first architecture

Problems, content, and progress sync when connected, but the core experience works on the tube, in a lift, and in areas with no signal.

Learnings

What this project taught me

Completion is an illusion

Long courses create a false finish line that makes any interruption feel like failure. Daily tracks reframe the goal: not “finish the course” but “show up today.”

The plateau is a design problem

The motivation collapse at 30–40% is predictable and preventable. Designing for it specifically keeps users moving through the hardest stretch.

Confidence is a metric too

Standardised assessment scores don’t tell you whether someone feels ready. Confidence tracked more closely with actual interview performance than problem-solve rates did.

Offline is not an edge case

Treating connectivity as optional rather than required changed how we thought about the entire product architecture. Features that require a connection are features some users will never use.

MAI Labs — Company Website

A design that doesn’t reach code is still just a hypothesis. This project was about closing the gap — from Figma to deployed, fully responsible for both.

Role
UI Developer & Consultant
Platform
Web (Desktop & Mobile)
Duration
3 Months
Tools
Next.js, Tailwind CSS, GitHub, Vercel

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Design and develop the complete MAI Labs company website from the ground up — fully responsive, performant across browsers, and built with a reusable component system that the internal team could maintain and extend after handoff.

“Every time we try to update the site, something else breaks. We’ve just stopped updating it.”

The Problem

A site the team can’t own

This was a different kind of design challenge. There was no user problem to diagnose — there was a craft problem. The existing site was slow, visually inconsistent, and couldn’t be updated without breaking things. The brief was: build something the team can own.

Key pain points

  • No component system — every page was custom-coded, making updates expensive
  • No mobile experience — layout broke below 1200px
  • Page load time over 6 seconds due to unoptimised assets
  • Contact and application forms sent emails to a personal inbox with no structure
  • Brand inconsistency across pages — multiple type scales and button styles

Research

Understanding before building

Codebase Audit

Reviewed the existing site for architectural issues, dependency bloat, and performance bottlenecks. A full rebuild would be faster and more maintainable than attempting to refactor.

Stakeholder Interviews

Three sessions with the founding team to understand the site’s goals, the team’s technical comfort level for future maintenance, and the content they most needed to communicate.

Component Inventory

Mapped all UI patterns needed across the site to design a minimal, complete component library before writing a single line of production code.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Design the component system first

Before any page layout, designed and agreed on the complete component library: buttons, cards, navigation, forms, and typography tokens.

02

Build with Next.js App Router

Chose Next.js for server-side rendering, built-in image optimisation, and file-based routing that makes future page additions intuitive for non-engineers.

03

Tailwind utility system

Utility-first approach means any team member can read and update styles without understanding the full CSS architecture.

04

API-integrated forms

Built contact and job application forms with server-side API routes, validation, and structured email templates. Applications arrive formatted and filterable.

05

Performance and cross-browser QA

Lighthouse audits on every page. Compressed and converted all assets to WebP. Tested across Chrome, Safari, Firefox, and Edge on desktop and mobile.

Learnings

What this project taught me

Maintainability is a design output

A site the team can’t update will stop being updated. Designing for the person who maintains the product after you leave is as important as designing for the end user.

Component-first is faster, not slower

Spending a week on the component system before building pages felt like overhead. It saved three weeks of inconsistency fixes and rework in the final sprint.

Performance is a feature

A 6-second load time is a worse first impression than bad visual design. Performance optimisation is UX work — it just lives in the build system, not Figma.

Document for the person after you

The handoff documentation included not just “how to add a page” but “why certain architecture decisions were made.” Future developers need context, not just instructions.

AccessIQ — WCAG Summariser

Accessibility audits are only useful if teams can read them. AccessIQ translates WCAG compliance findings into plain language — closing the gap between audit and action.

Role
Designer & Developer
Platform
Web App
Duration
Side Project
Tools
Figma, React, OpenAI API, Tailwind CSS

Overview

The brief in one sentence

Build a tool that makes WCAG 2.1 compliance findings understandable for design and development teams who have the will to fix accessibility issues but lack the knowledge to interpret technical audit reports.

“We got an audit back with 47 issues. I read the first three, had no idea what they meant practically, and forwarded it to a developer who also had no idea.”

The Problem

It’s a translation problem, not a willingness problem

The accessibility problem in most organisations isn’t malice or laziness. It’s translation. Audit reports come back written in WCAG criterion language that means nothing to a designer who’s never read the spec. The findings sit in a spreadsheet and nothing changes.

Key pain points

  • WCAG criterion codes (e.g. “1.4.3 Contrast Minimum AA”) are meaningless without deep knowledge of the spec
  • Audit reports list issues without design-specific remediation guidance
  • No differentiation between issues affecting different user groups
  • No prioritisation of issues by severity and actual user impact
  • Designers and developers need different guidance for the same issue

Research

Understanding the gap

Designer Interviews

Spoke with 8 product designers at companies that had recently received accessibility audits. Mapped how reports were read, who they were forwarded to, and how many issues were actually resolved.

Specialist Consultation

Consulted with two certified accessibility specialists to understand which WCAG criteria are most commonly misunderstood, and what plain-language framing works best for each category.

Audit Report Analysis

Collected and analysed 12 real accessibility audit reports from open-source projects. Categorised findings by criterion type, severity, and how actionable the guidance was.

Design Process

From insight to interface

01

Plain-language criterion library

Built a complete library of all Level A and AA WCAG 2.1 criteria, each translated into three versions: a one-sentence summary, a designer-specific guide, and a developer-specific fix guide.

02

AI interpretation layer

Integrated OpenAI’s API to take raw audit output and map findings to the criterion library, generating a structured, plain-language action plan automatically.

03

Role-based remediation views

Separate output views for designers and developers. The same issue surfaces different guidance depending on who is reading it.

04

Severity and impact scoring

Custom severity model that scores each issue on both WCAG level and estimated user impact across disability categories, allowing teams to prioritise by real-world effect.

05

Shareable report output

Clean, exportable report format that product managers can share with stakeholders as a readable brief rather than a technical spreadsheet.

Accessibility is a communication problem

The biggest barrier to accessible products isn’t technical complexity. It’s the translation gap between compliance language and design practice.

Role-specific guidance multiplies action

Splitting the same issue into “what the designer does” and “what the developer does” eliminated the round-trip conversation that usually results in neither person acting.

Build the thing you wish existed

This tool came from a real frustration encountered in professional work. The most useful tools are often the ones you build because you couldn’t find them anywhere else.

Practise what you preach

Building an accessibility tool with an inaccessible interface would be the worst possible outcome. Achieving a perfect Lighthouse accessibility score was non-negotiable.
